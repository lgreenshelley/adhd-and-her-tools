A few important things to know

About this tool

This is an educational and self-exploration resource created by Shelley L'Green, an Occupational Therapist (AHPRA registered). It is designed to support self-awareness and understanding of sensory preferences in the context of ADHD and nervous system regulation.

It is not a clinical assessment, diagnostic tool, or substitute for professional advice. Using this tool does not create a therapeutic relationship between you and Shelley. If you are experiencing significant distress, please seek support from a qualified health professional.

Who this is for

This tool is designed for adults aged 18 and over who identify as women or gender diverse, who have a diagnosis of ADHD, are exploring the possibility of ADHD, or who are simply curious about how their sensory system and nervous system work together. It uses affirming, non-pathologising language throughout. A separate edition for younger ages is in development.

Your data and privacy

This tool runs entirely on your device. Your responses are not collected, stored, or transmitted anywhere. Nothing you enter leaves your browser. When you close the file, your answers are gone unless you have printed or saved your worksheet as a PDF.

This tool involves paying attention to your body and nervous system. If that ever feels overwhelming, it is completely fine to pause and return when you are ready. This tool is not intended for use during a mental health crisis.

Introduction
Before you begin

Everyone has sensory preferences

This is an invitation to explore how your nervous system works, with curiosity, not judgment. There are no right or wrong answers here. Everything you notice is useful information.

If you have ever felt too much, not enough, or just wired differently to the people around you, you are not imagining it. Research consistently shows that people with ADHD experience the sensory world differently than average. This can show up as being more easily overwhelmed by sensory input, needing more of it to feel alert and focused, or both at the same time. Understanding how your nervous system takes in and responds to sensory input is genuinely useful — and it is one of the most accessible places to start. It is not the whole picture. Sleep, stress, relationships, hormones, and emotional load all shape your nervous system too. But sensory input is something you can actually do something about, day to day. This questionnaire is a starting point.

Questionnaire
Sensory Preferences Questionnaire

How does your sensory world show up day to day?

For each statement below, choose how often it applies to you. There are no right answers, just honest ones.

Use this guide when answering:

Almost never: rarely, less than 10% of the time
Sometimes: roughly 25 to 50% of the time
Often: roughly 50 to 75% of the time
Almost always: 75% or more of the time

Answer based on how you generally experience life, not just today. If it varies with your hormonal cycle or stress levels, go with your most common experience.

Domain 1 of 8

Tactile: touch and texture

How your skin and body registers touch including its type, texture, temperature, and predictability. Tactile differences are often present from early childhood and frequently misread as behavioural difficulties.

I am very particular about clothing. Tags, seams, fabrics, or fit can make a garment completely unwearable for me.

Unexpected touch from others, even in affectionate contexts, can feel jarring, intrusive, or unpleasant.

I love certain textures and seek them out, like soft blankets, smooth surfaces, or fidget tools, for comfort and focus.

Temperature sensitivity is significant for me. I am often too hot or too cold when others seem comfortable, and it affects my mood and focus.

👂
Domain 2 of 8

Auditory: how your nervous system responds to sound

How your brain registers and responds to sound, including its volume, frequency, predictability, and meaning. Sound sensitivity is one of the most commonly reported sensory experiences for people with ADHD.

I need background noise or music to concentrate. Silence feels uncomfortable or distracting.

I am easily startled by sudden sounds and it takes me time to settle afterwards.

Certain sounds (chewing, tapping, high-pitched tones) cause me strong irritation or even physical discomfort, beyond what others seem to experience.

In noisy environments (open offices, restaurants, parties), I struggle to filter background noise and find conversations exhausting.

👁️
Domain 3 of 8

Visual: light, movement and visual detail

How your brain processes visual information including brightness, contrast, movement, and visual clutter. Visual sensitivity is often underestimated as a source of fatigue and overwhelm.

Bright lights, especially fluorescent or overhead lights, quickly make me feel overstimulated, irritable, or fatigued.

I am drawn to visually stimulating environments: bright colours, lots of activity, interesting things to look at.

Clutter or visual mess in my environment makes it difficult for me to think clearly or feel calm.

I notice visual details that others miss (patterns, small changes in a room, facial expressions), often before I am consciously aware of it.

👃
Domain 4 of 8

Smell and taste

Smell and taste are technically separate sensory systems — olfactory and gustatory — but they are so closely connected in everyday experience that this questionnaire covers them together. How you register and respond to scent and flavour affects appetite, food choices, social comfort, and how safe or pleasant an environment feels. Both are closely linked to emotion and memory, which is why a particular smell can change how your body feels almost instantly.

Certain smells are intensely unpleasant for me, such as cleaning products, perfume, or food smells, and can quickly shift my mood or make me feel unwell.

I use scent intentionally to regulate my mood (candles, essential oils, a particular perfume) because smell has a powerful effect on how I feel.

I have a very limited range of foods I find tolerable. Textures or tastes that others barely notice can be genuinely distressing for me.

I seek out strong flavours or unusual food textures like very spicy, sour, or crunchy foods, and find them satisfying or energising.

🌀
Domain 5 of 8

Vestibular: movement and balance

The vestibular system lives in your inner ear and detects movement, head position, and balance. It is deeply connected to alertness, attention, and emotional regulation, making it one of the most influential sensory systems for nervous system state.

I feel most focused or calm when I am moving: rocking, bouncing, pacing, or swinging.

I crave fast, thrilling movement like roller coasters, spinning, running or dancing, and it feels regulating rather than scary.

Unexpected movement (a sudden jolt in the car, turbulence, an elevator stopping abruptly) makes me feel anxious or disoriented.

I feel motion sick easily (cars, boats, screens with moving images) and prefer stillness and predictability.

💪
Domain 6 of 8

Proprioception: body awareness and pressure

Proprioception tells your brain where your body is in space. It is activated by deep pressure, muscle resistance, and heavy work. Alongside vestibular input and interoception, it is one of the most reliable and accessible inputs for supporting nervous system regulation.

I love the feeling of tight clothing, weighted blankets, deep pressure massage, or being wrapped up. It feels calming.

I bump into things, misjudge distances, or feel clumsy, as though I cannot quite locate my body in space.

Light touch (a tag in a shirt, someone brushing past me) is far more uncomfortable than firm, expected pressure.

I use heavy work such as carrying bags, pushing furniture, or intense exercise to feel grounded and calm.

🫀
Domain 7 of 8

Interoception: your body's inner signals

Interoception is your ability to sense what is happening inside your body: hunger, thirst, heartbeat, temperature, the need to use the bathroom, and emotional feelings in the body. It is considered foundational to nervous system regulation.

I need strong physical sensations (intense exercise, very hot baths, spicy food) to feel present in my body.

I often don't notice I am hungry, thirsty, or tired until it becomes extreme (I forget to eat, or I crash suddenly).

Physical sensations in my body (heartbeat, stomach feelings, breathing) can quickly feel alarming or overwhelming.

I feel my emotions very intensely as physical sensations (tight chest, sick stomach, hot face) before I even name the feeling.

🫶
Domain 8 of 8

Social and emotional sensory environment

This domain acknowledges that social environments carry sensory load. Other people's energy, facial expressions, emotional tone, and the demands of masking are all processed by and felt in your nervous system, often registering as physical sensations before you have named what is happening.

I absorb other people's emotions strongly. If someone near me is anxious, angry, or upset, I feel it physically in my own body.

After a period of masking or performing in social situations, I need significant time alone to recover. I am often more depleted than the situation "should" warrant.

Busy, unpredictable social environments (parties, crowded spaces, group events) feel more overwhelming than others seem to find them.

I tend to be highly attuned to subtle shifts in other people (tone of voice, body language, mood changes), often noticing before others do and finding it activating.

Understanding your nervous system

⏱ 8–10 min read

Now that you have seen your results, these two ideas will help them make much more sense — and make your tools and tips easier to use. You do not need to memorise any of it. Just let it land.

Part 1 of 2

Your window of capacity

Most of us have felt it. That agitated, can't-settle feeling when everything is too loud and too much. Or the heaviness that makes getting off the couch feel genuinely impossible — not lazy, just completely flat. Your nervous system was doing something specific in both of those moments. This framework puts words to it.

Your nervous system moves between three energy states throughout the day. And this is something nobody taught us in school, even though it shapes every single moment of our lives.

Above your window
Hyperactivated — high energy state
Racing, restless, flooded, reactive, on edge. Useful for urgency and performance — difficult when stuck here.
Inside your window
Optimal energy zone
Present, grounded, connected. Able to think, feel, and respond with ease.
Below your window
Hypoactivated — low energy state
Heavy, flat, foggy, withdrawn, numb. Useful for rest and recovery — difficult when stuck here.

The first is your optimal energy zone — inside your window of capacity. You feel like yourself here. Present, grounded, connected. Life feels manageable. You can think clearly, make decisions without everything feeling loaded, and handle the ordinary demands of the day with some degree of ease.

The second is a high-energy, hyperactivated state — above your window. This is your nervous system mobilising energy, flooding your body with urgency. Your heart races. Your thoughts won't slow down. You might go reactive, snappy, restless. Spiralling. Can't switch off. Can't stop. Sometimes this state is exactly what you need — it is what gets you through a deadline, a performance, a moment that demands everything you have. The difficulty is getting stuck there long after the moment has passed.

The third is a low-energy, hypoactivated state — below your window. This is your system shutting down. Heavy. Flat. Foggy. Like watching life from behind glass, knowing you want to engage but not being able to get there. This state has its place too — it is how your body rests and recovers. The difficulty is getting stuck there when life needs you to show up.

When you are stuck in a hyperactivated state, you might notice a racing mind, irritability, difficulty switching off, restlessness, or a sense of doing too much and being unable to stop — even when part of you desperately wants to.

When you are stuck in a hypoactivated state, you might notice feeling foggy, numb, flat, heavy, or like you are watching life from behind glass.

Regulation is not about staying in one place. It is the ability to move between states as life demands, and to find your way back when you have been stuck somewhere longer than you would like. Awareness is where that starts.

Note: fawn responses — appeasing, people-pleasing, or over-accommodating to reduce perceived threat — can appear in either state. They are the nervous system trying to create safety through behaviour rather than fight, flight, or shutdown.

ADHD and your window

For women with ADHD, moving between states and finding the way back is often more pronounced. The ADHD nervous system is not broken, but it is wired differently. The same neurological differences that affect attention and executive function also affect how quickly sensory and emotional input shifts the system outside its window.

Hormones compound this further. Oestrogen supports dopamine availability in the brain. For women with ADHD, whose dopamine regulation is already different, a drop in oestrogen means a drop in dopamine too. This is why the week before menstruation and perimenopause can feel so significant: it is not just one system under strain, it is two. Sensory sensitivity increases, the window narrows, and executive function becomes harder still. What feels manageable on a Tuesday in the first half of your cycle can feel genuinely overwhelming on the same Tuesday the week before your period. This is not fragility. It is physiology. The tools and tips section covers cycle-specific supports in detail.

This is also why understanding your sensory preferences is not just interesting — it is practical. Which is exactly where Part 2 comes in.

Part 2 of 2

Your sensory preferences

So your nervous system shifts between states. Now here is what shapes that shifting — and why sensory input is so central to it.

Everyone has sensory preferences. They are not a sign that something is wrong with you. They are simply information about how your nervous system is wired, and understanding them is one of the most practical things you can do for your own wellbeing.

Your sensory system and your nervous system state are not two separate things. The same neural pathways that process sensory input also regulate how activated or calm you feel. When your sensory environment does not match what your nervous system needs — too much, too little, or the wrong kind for where you are right now — it adds to the activation load your system is already managing. It is worth saying clearly: sensory input is not the only thing that shifts your nervous system. Sleep, stress, emotional load, relationships, and hormones all play a role. This tool focuses on the sensory piece because it has a significant impact on nervous system regulation and is often underrecognised.

This is where the two frameworks meet. Your sensory preferences tell you something useful in both directions: what tends to shift your system outside your window and keep it there, and what can help you shift back.

The Sensory Cup Analogy

Think of each of your sensory systems as having its own cup. The size of that cup is your threshold — how much input that system needs before it feels satisfied, or before it overflows. A large cup means you need a lot of input to feel settled (sensory seeking). A small cup means your system fills quickly and overflows easily (sensory sensitive). Most people with ADHD have a mix of both across different systems — which is why you might crave movement and noise in one context and be easily overwhelmed by sound or light in another. Both are real. Both make sense.

Your cup size also shifts day to day. Masking, poor sleep, hormonal changes, or emotional stress can all temporarily lower your threshold — meaning sensory input that usually feels manageable can suddenly feel like too much. This is not inconsistency. It is your nervous system communicating its current capacity.

How this works in practice

Your sensory preferences as a regulation tool

Now you can put both ideas to work.

Sensory input that does not match what your nervous system needs can add to the activation load your system is already managing, and is often part of what keeps you stuck outside your window.

Your sensory preferences can also point you toward the inputs and strategies that help your nervous system shift — down from a high-energy state, or up from a low-energy one.

The direction matters. In a high-energy state, you are looking to slow and settle. In a low-energy state, you are looking to build activation and re-engage. Knowing your state first helps you choose a tool that actually matches what your nervous system needs in that moment.

Above your window · ↓ Down-regulate
Hyperactivated — high energy state

Activated, reactive, overwhelmed, on edge. Slow and settle your nervous system.

Inside your window
Inside your window

Present, connected, able to think and respond. This is where the tools are aiming to bring you.

Below your window · ↑ Up-regulate

Choose based on where you are right now. A tool that works for someone else may not work for you — which is exactly why understanding your own sensory preferences matters.

Putting it together: meet Jane

Combined seeker and sensitive pattern

Jane is a fictional character created to illustrate how sensory preferences, interoceptive awareness, and the window of capacity work together in everyday life.

Jane is a sensory seeker in her vestibular and proprioceptive systems. She needs movement and deep pressure as part of her everyday sensory toolkit to support her nervous system. She is also sensitive in her auditory and visual systems, meaning loud, unpredictable sound and bright overhead lighting are genuinely draining for her nervous system.

On a typical workday, Jane sits through four hours of back-to-back meetings in a bright open-plan office. She has had limited opportunity to move her body, no deep pressure input, and has been managing constant auditory and visual stimulation that is difficult for her to filter. By mid-afternoon, Jane notices she feels irritable, unsettled, anxious, and fidgety. She cannot sit still and is snapping at small things.

She pauses and checks in with herself. Using her interoceptive awareness — her ability to notice sensations inside her body — she notices: racing thoughts, extra fidgeting, easily frustrated, a tight chest, and a tense jaw. She recognises these as signs that her nervous system has shifted into a hyperactivated state — a high-energy place. She needs to down-regulate. Using her sensory toolkit as a guide, she chooses:

Jane's down-regulation tools
  • A walk around the block before heading to her car: vestibular, proprioceptive, and calming visual input
  • Calming music to hum along to on the drive home: predictable, soothing auditory input
  • Chewing crunchy crackers: organising proprioceptive input through the jaw
  • Long, slow exhales: calming the nervous system through slow, controlled breathing

By the time Jane arrives home, she can feel her nervous system beginning to shift. She is not fully back inside her window yet, but she is no longer stuck in that high-energy place. That small shift is enough to arrive differently.

Jane did not need to do everything. She noticed her state, identified what her nervous system was responding to, chose a few tools that matched, and felt the shift begin. That is what regulation looks like in practice — and it started with knowing herself a little better.

Using what you have discovered

Two ways your sensory preferences can help

⏱ 2 min read

Your sensory preferences are not a diagnosis. They are a starting point. Here is one way to think about how to use what you have discovered.

Understanding your sensory preferences can guide how you respond to your nervous system in two different ways. They are not separate things. They work together over time.

Layer 1: in the moment

Noticing and responding to state shifts

When you notice your nervous system has shifted — or is starting to shift — your sensory environment may be part of what contributed to that. It may not be the only factor, and sometimes it will not be the main one. But it is often a thread worth pulling. Your sensory preferences can also point you toward what might help you shift back. This is not a formula. Your interoceptive signals, the context you are in, and how much energy, time, and practical access you have in the moment all matter too.

The tools and tips in the previous section serve both layers. Some are in-the-moment tools you can reach for when you notice a state shift. Others are proactive practices and environmental adjustments that reduce the load your nervous system carries day to day. Both matter.

↓ Down-regulate
Above your window

Slow and settle a high-energy, flooded, or reactive nervous system.

↑ Up-regulate
Below your window

Gently activate a flat, foggy, or disconnected nervous system and build energy to re-engage.

Layer 2: proactive and ongoing

Widening your window over time

The window of capacity is not fixed. With awareness and the right conditions, it can widen over time. This is the deeper work, and it goes beyond sensory tools alone.

Understanding your sensory preferences can help you design your environment and daily life in ways that keep your nervous system better resourced from the start. A nervous system that begins each day with less cumulative load has more capacity to draw on when things get hard.

Environmental design

Lighting, sound, texture, and space — shaped around your sensory preferences

Daily nervous system inputs

Movement, nutrition, sleep, sunlight, connection, and community

Beliefs and self-story

Examining the narratives that keep the window narrow and updating them over time

Executive functioning and routines

Structures that reduce the daily demand on your nervous system

This tool offers a starting point for Layer 1. Layer 2 is a longer and more personal conversation. An occupational therapist who understands ADHD and sensory processing can help you explore both layers in the context of your actual life, environment, relationships, and daily demands.
